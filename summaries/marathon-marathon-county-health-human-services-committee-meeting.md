## Meeting Overview
The Marathon County Health & Human Services Committee held an orientation meeting for new board members, featuring educational presentations from department heads on the Health Department, Social Services, Veteran Services, and Aging Disability Resource Center. The committee approved April 1st meeting minutes unanimously and received detailed briefings on department missions, budgets, and current initiatives including homelessness response, veteran suicide prevention, and challenges with nutrition program funding.

## Key Discussions
### Approval of April 1st meeting minutes
Supervisor Cavelli made a motion to approve the minutes, seconded by Vice Chair Rosenberg. The motion passed unanimously with all members voting in favor.

### Standing Committee Ordinance Review
Administrator Lance Leonard explained the committee's jurisdiction under section 2.04 sub5, covering departments including Veteran Services, Social Services, and Health Department, plus affiliated entities North Central Health Care and Aging Disability Resource Center. He outlined relationships with various boards including the Social Services Board, Veteran Service Commission, Transportation Coordinating Committee, and Board of Health. Chair Boots noted the strategic plan development will be a focus through December, with meetings aligned to strategic objectives.

### Health Department Presentation
Health Officer Caitlyn Suginaka presented the department's mission of preventing disease, promoting health, and protecting from environmental hazards. The department has 37.6 FTE across four teams: environmental health, nursing, community health improvement, and administration. Key initiatives include public health vending machines with free tick kits and harm reduction supplies, the Nurse Family Partnership program with first graduates (two-year-olds), and a new homeless system response position. The department manages over 1,200 licenses and has measles response plans in place. Supervisor Kremes asked about pandemic preparedness, and Suginaka confirmed extensive training, response plans, and staffing strategies developed from COVID experience.

### Social Services Department Overview
Administrator Leonard presented on behalf of Director Christa, noting the department has nearly 140 FTE with a 2026 budget over $22 million, of which approximately $7 million is tax levy. He highlighted the Family Keys housing stability pilot program, developed with state funding and county board input, which addresses root causes of housing instability. The department covers child welfare, youth justice, children's long-term support, economic support, and child support. Leonard indicated upcoming requests for restructuring within units due to increased need.

### Veteran Service Department Presentation
Veteran Service Officer Erin Galindo reported the department grew from 2.75 to 3.0 FTE and saw customer interactions increase from approximately 5,900 to 8,149 in 2025. Veterans in Marathon County receive $44 million annually in benefits, with $2.6 million in retroactive payments in 2025. Key initiatives include suicide prevention efforts (7 of 30 county suicides in 2025 were veterans), housing stability partnerships with Center for Veterans Issues, and PACT Act claims processing. The department is working with hospice facilities and the medical examiner's office to improve death certificate documentation for surviving spouse benefits. Supervisor Kremes asked about regional collaboration; Galindo confirmed good working relationships with neighboring counties while noting state statute requires each county to maintain its own VSO.

### Aging Disability Resource Center Presentation
Executive Director Mike Ray presented on the four-county regional ADRC serving Marathon, Lincoln, Langlade, and Wood counties. The organization has about 60 FTE and 385-400 volunteers, with 11% county levy funding. Major challenges include nutrition program costs rising 40-45% over four years while federal Older Americans Act funding remains flat at 2024 levels. The organization hadn't requested a levy increase from 2011 until 2024. Services include Meals on Wheels, Medicare/Medicaid benefit specialists, dementia care specialists, and falls prevention programs. Wisconsin is number one nationally in falls and falls-related injuries. Ray noted staff have had to reduce meals served, delivery days, and staffing to address budget constraints.
