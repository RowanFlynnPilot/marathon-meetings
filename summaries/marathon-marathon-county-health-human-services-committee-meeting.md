## Meeting Overview
The Marathon County Health & Human Services Committee held an educational orientation meeting for new board members, featuring presentations from the Health Department, Social Services, Veteran Service Office, and Aging Disability Resource Center. No action items were voted on aside from approving previous meeting minutes. The meeting focused on familiarizing committee members with department structures, budgets, and services.

## Key Discussions
### Approval of April 1st meeting minutes
Supervisor Cavelli made a motion to approve the minutes. Vice Chair Rosenberg seconded. The motion carried unanimously with all in favor.

### Health Department presentation
Health Officer Caitlyn Suginaka presented an overview of the department's mission and services, including environmental health licensing (over 1,200 licenses), communicable disease prevention, family health programs like Nurse Family Partnership, and the new homeless system response position. She highlighted public health vending machines stocked with free resources like tick kits. The department has approximately 37.6 FTE with about half of its $5 million budget funded by tax levy and the rest through grants and fees.

### Social Services Department overview
Administrator Leonard presented on behalf of Director Christa who was unavailable. The department has nearly 140 FTE with a 2026 budget of over $22 million, of which about $7 million is tax levy. He highlighted the Family Keys housing stability program, a successful state pilot addressing housing instability for families in child welfare and youth justice. He noted potential future requests for restructuring within the department due to increased service needs.

### Veteran Service Office presentation
Veteran Service Officer Aaron Galindo reported his office increased to 3.0 FTE in 2025 and had 8,149 client interactions, up from 5,900 the previous year. He noted $44 million annually flows to Marathon County veterans, with $2.6 million in retroactive payments in 2025. Key initiatives include surviving spouse benefits outreach, housing stability partnerships with Center for Veterans Issues, and suicide prevention efforts (7 of 30 county suicides in 2025 were veterans). Supervisor Kremes asked about regional coordination; Galindo explained good working relationships exist with neighboring counties but state statute requires each county to have its own VSO.

### Aging Disability Resource Center presentation
Executive Director Mike Ray explained the ADRC serves four counties (Marathon, Lincoln, Langlade, Wood) with about 60 FTE and 385-400 volunteers. Marathon County's contribution is $413,135. He described services including information and assistance, caregiver support, dementia care, falls prevention, and Meals on Wheels. Ray noted significant financial challenges as nutrition program costs increased 40-45% over four years while federal funding remained flat, forcing meal and staffing reductions.
