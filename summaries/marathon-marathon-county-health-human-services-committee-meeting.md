## Meeting Overview
The Marathon County Health & Human Services Committee held an educational orientation meeting for new board members, featuring presentations from department heads on the Health Department, Social Services, Veteran Service Office, and Aging Disability Resource Center. No formal policy votes were taken; the meeting focused on introducing committee members to department missions, structures, budgets, and current initiatives.

## Key Discussions
### Approval of April 1st meeting minutes
Supervisor Cavelli made a motion to approve the minutes, seconded by Vice Chair Rosenberg. The motion passed unanimously with all in favor.

### Committee structure and ordinances overview
Administrator Lance Leonard explained the committee's jurisdiction under ordinance section 2.04 sub5, covering the Health Department, Social Services, Veteran Service Office, and affiliated entities including North Central Health Care and the Aging Disability Resource Center. He outlined reporting relationships with statutory boards including the Social Services Board, Veteran Service Commission, Transportation Coordinating Committee, and Board of Health. Chair Boots noted the upcoming strategic plan development expected by December.

### Health Department presentation
Health Officer Caitlyn Suginaka presented the department's mission of preventing disease, promoting health, and protecting from environmental hazards. She highlighted over 1,200 licenses overseen for restaurants, pools, and hotels; communicable disease response including recent tick kit distribution through free public health vending machines; the Nurse Family Partnership program now graduating its first two-year-old cohort; and a new FTE position for homeless system response. The department has approximately 37.6 FTE staff across four divisions: environmental health, nursing, community health improvement, and administration. Administrator Leonard noted about half the $5 million budget is tax levy, with the rest from public charges for services and intergovernmental grants.

### Social Services Department overview
Administrator Leonard presented on behalf of Director Christa who was unavailable. The department has nearly 140 FTE handling child welfare, youth justice, children's long-term support, economic support, and child support. Of their $22 million budget, approximately $7 million is tax levy. Leonard highlighted the successful Family Keys housing stability pilot program addressing housing instability root causes, noting many participants had never had bank accounts or formal leases before. He indicated upcoming restructuring requests may come forward due to increased need in certain units.

### Veteran Service Office presentation
VSO Aaron Galindo reported his office of 3.0 FTE had 8,149 interactions in 2025, up significantly from prior years. He highlighted $44 million annually in VA benefits coming to county veterans, plus $2.6 million in retroactive payments in 2025. Key initiatives include surviving spouse benefits outreach, housing stability work with Center for Veterans Issues establishing a local office, and suicide prevention efforts noting 7 of 30 county suicides in 2025 were veterans. Galindo confirmed the office can help any veteran regardless of county residence due to federal accreditation. Supervisor Kremes asked about regional collaboration; Galindo explained state statute requires each county to have a VSO and his office maintains good relationships with neighboring counties using compatible systems like VetPro.

### Aging Disability Resource Center presentation
Executive Director Mike Ray presented on the four-county ADRC serving Marathon, Lincoln, Langlade, and Wood counties. About 11% of their budget comes from county levy. He described services including information and assistance, dementia care specialists, falls prevention through the 'Stepping On' program, Medicare/Medicaid benefit specialists, and the Meals on Wheels nutrition program. Ray emphasized significant financial challenges with nutrition program costs up 40-45% in four years while federal Older Americans Act funding remains flat at 2024 levels. The organization has approximately 75 staff plus 385-400 volunteers. The ADRC had not requested a levy increase from 2011 until 2024. Vice Chair Rosenberg praised the culturally appropriate meal program at the Hmong American Center.
