## Meeting Overview
The Marathon County Health & Human Services Committee held an orientation meeting for new board members, featuring presentations from the Health Department, Social Services, Veterans Service Office, and Aging Disability Resource Center. No formal votes were taken beyond approving meeting minutes; the meeting focused on educating committee members about departmental missions, budgets, and key programs.

## Key Discussions
### Approval of April 1st meeting minutes
Supervisor Cavalli moved to approve the minutes, Vice Chair Rosenberg seconded. The motion passed unanimously with voice vote.

### Health Department presentation
Health Officer Caitlyn Suginaka presented the department's mission of preventing disease, promoting health, and protecting from environmental hazards. She highlighted the department's 37.6 FTE staff across four divisions, over 1,200 licenses overseen, public health vending machines stocked with free tick kits and harm reduction supplies, the Nurse Family Partnership program now graduating its first two-year-olds, and a new FTE position for homeless system response. About half of their $5 million budget comes from tax levy, with significant funding from intergovernmental grants and public charges for services.

### Social Services Department overview
Administrator Leonard presented on behalf of Director Christa who was unavailable. The department has nearly 140 FTE and a budget over $22 million with about $7 million in tax levy. He highlighted the successful Family Keys pilot program addressing housing instability for child welfare and youth justice families, noting many participants had never had bank accounts or been on a formal lease. He mentioned potential restructuring requests coming for increased staffing needs in children's long-term support and child support units.

### Veterans Service Office presentation
Veteran Service Officer Aaron Galindo reported the office increased from 2.75 to 3.0 FTE and interactions jumped from 5,900 in 2024 to 8,149 in 2025. He noted $44 million in annual VA benefits comes into the county, with $2.6 million in retroactive payments in 2025. Key initiatives include working with surviving spouses on dependency and indemnity compensation benefits (up to $2,400/month tax-free), partnering with Center for Veterans Issues for housing stability, and addressing veteran suicide prevention after 7 of 30 county suicides in 2025 were veterans. Supervisor Kremes asked about regional coordination; Galindo explained state statute requires each county to have its own VSO but they collaborate across county lines.

### Aging Disability Resource Center presentation
Executive Director Mike Ray explained the ADRC serves four counties (Marathon, Lincoln, Langlade, Wood) as a one-stop shop for aging and disability services. The organization has about 60 FTE plus 385-400 volunteers, with only 11% of budget from county levy. He highlighted significant financial challenges with nutrition/Meals on Wheels program, noting food costs increased 40-45% over four years while federal Older Americans Act funding remains flat at 2024 levels. The organization hadn't requested a levy increase from 2011 until 2024, demonstrating the regional model's efficiency.

### Strategic Plan discussion
Chair Boots noted the county recently finished the comprehensive plan and will be developing a new strategic plan targeting completion by December. He indicated future meetings will track progress on strategic plan objectives relevant to this committee.
