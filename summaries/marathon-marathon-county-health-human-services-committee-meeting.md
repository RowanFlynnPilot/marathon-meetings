## Meeting Overview
The Marathon County Health & Human Services Committee held an educational orientation meeting for new and returning members, featuring presentations from department heads covering the Health Department, Social Services, Veteran Service Office, and Aging Disability Resource Center. The committee approved April 1st meeting minutes unanimously and received comprehensive overviews of department missions, budgets, and current initiatives including homelessness response efforts and veteran services.

## Key Discussions
### Approval of April 1st Meeting Minutes
Supervisor Cavelli made a motion to approve the minutes, seconded by Vice Chair Rosenberg. The motion passed unanimously with all members voting in favor.

### Review of Standing Committee Ordinances
Administrator Lance Leonard provided an overview of the committee's structure under section 2.04 sub5 of county ordinances. He explained reporting relationships with three direct county departments (Veteran Service, Social Services, Health Department) and affiliated entities (North Central Health Care, Aging Disability Resource Center). Chair Boots noted the committee will be working on a new strategic plan to be completed by December.

### Health Department Overview
Health Officer Caitlyn Suginaka presented on the department's mission to prevent disease, promote health, and protect from environmental hazards. She highlighted over 1,200 licenses overseen, public health vending machines providing free resources including tick kits, the Nurse Family Partnership program now graduating its first two-year-olds, and a new FTE position for homeless system response. The department has approximately 37.6 FTE and about half of its $5 million budget comes from tax levy. Supervisor Kremes asked about pandemic preparedness, and Suginaka confirmed extensive plans are in place with staff trained from COVID experience.

### Social Services Department Overview
Administrator Leonard presented on behalf of Director Christa who could not attend. He covered the department's divisions including child welfare, youth justice, children's long-term support, economic support, and child support. The department has nearly 140 FTE with a 2026 revenue budget over $22 million and about $7 million in tax levy. Leonard highlighted the successful Family Keys program addressing housing instability, which was piloted after state outreach and modified based on board feedback.

### Veteran Service Office Overview
Veteran Service Officer Aaron Galindo reported his office increased to 3.0 FTE in 2025. Customer interactions jumped from approximately 5,900 in early 2025 to 8,149 by year end. He reported $44 million annually in benefits coming to local veterans and $2.6 million in retroactive payments in 2025. Galindo discussed housing stability initiatives with Center for Veterans Issues and suicide prevention efforts, noting Marathon County had 30 suicides in 2025 with 7 being veterans. Supervisor Kremes asked about inter-county coordination, and Galindo explained veterans can work with any county's VSO and his office has good relationships with neighboring counties.

### Aging Disability Resource Center Overview
Executive Director Mike Ray presented on the four-county ADRC serving Marathon, Lincoln, Langlade, and Wood counties with approximately 60 FTE and 385-400 volunteers. He highlighted significant financial challenges with nutrition program costs up 40-45% over four years while federal funding remained flat. The Older Americans Act hasn't been reauthorized in over two years. Marathon County's contribution is $413,135. Ray noted the organization hadn't requested a levy increase from 2011 until 2024. Vice Chair Rosenberg praised the culturally appropriate meals program at the Hmong American Center.
