## Meeting Overview
The Marathon County Health & Human Services Committee held an educational orientation meeting for new board members, featuring presentations from department heads on their missions, budgets, and operations. No action items were voted on except approval of April 1st meeting minutes, which passed unanimously.

## Key Discussions
### Approval of April 1st meeting minutes
Supervisor Cavelli made a motion to approve the minutes. Vice Chair Rosenberg seconded. The motion passed unanimously with all in favor.

### Review of standing committee ordinances and structure
Administrator Lance Leonard explained the committee's jurisdiction over the Health Department, Social Services, and Veteran Services, plus affiliated entities North Central Health Care and the Aging Disability Resource Center. He outlined reporting relationships with statutory boards including the Social Services Board, Veteran Service Commission, Transportation Coordinating Committee, and Board of Health. Chair Boots noted the committee will focus on developing a new strategic plan by December 2025.

### Health Department presentation
Health Officer Caitlyn Suginaka presented on the department's mission of preventing disease and protecting public health. She highlighted 1,200 licenses overseen for restaurants, pools, and hotels; communicable disease prevention including recent tick awareness efforts; the Nurse Family Partnership program for pregnant mothers; and a new FTE position for homeless system response created following a county task force. The department has approximately 37.6 FTE staff. Administrator Leonard noted the department's $5 million budget is roughly half tax levy, with significant intergovernmental grants and public charges for environmental health services.

### Social Services Department overview
Administrator Leonard provided an overview in the director's absence, explaining the department's divisions: child welfare (child protective services and youth justice), children's long-term support, economic support, and child support. With nearly 140 FTE, it's the county's second-largest department. He highlighted the successful Family Keys pilot program addressing housing instability for child welfare and youth justice cases, noting the program substantively changed based on board feedback and has helped families who never had credit accounts or formal leases.

### Veteran Service Department presentation
VSO Aaron Galindo reported his office grew to 3.0 FTE in 2025 and saw customer interactions increase from 5,900 to 8,149. He noted $44 million in annual VA compensation flows to Marathon County veterans, with $2.6 million in retroactive payments in 2025. Galindo highlighted housing stability efforts through partnership with Center for Veterans Issues and suicide prevention initiatives, noting 7 of Marathon County's 30 suicides in 2025 were veterans. Supervisor Kremes asked about regional cooperation; Galindo explained state statute requires each county have a VSO but veterans can work with any office. He acknowledged staffing challenges, noting he was booking 6 weeks out when short-staffed.

### Aging Disability Resource Center presentation
Executive Director Mike Ray explained the ADRC serves four counties (Marathon, Lincoln, Langlade, Wood) as a one-stop shop for aging and disability services. Programs include information and assistance, caregiver support, dementia care, falls prevention, benefit specialists, and Meals on Wheels nutrition services. He reported significant financial challenges with federal funding flat since 2024 while nutrition costs increased 40-45% over four years, forcing reduced meals, service days, and staffing. Marathon County's levy contribution is $413,135. Vice Chair Rosenberg praised the culturally appropriate meals program at the Hmong American Center.
