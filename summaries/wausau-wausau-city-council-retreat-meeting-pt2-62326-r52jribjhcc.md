## Meeting Overview
The Wausau City Council held a strategic planning retreat where council members shared their top priorities for the 2026-2028 term, focusing heavily on economic development, housing affordability, fleet facility construction, transit expansion, and youth retention. The meeting concluded with a motion, passed unanimously, directing staff coordinator Michelle to compile all priority input from sticky notes and dot-voting exercises into a digestible format for distribution by end of week, after which the council will formally act on identifying the top three priorities.

## Key Discussions
### Fleet Facility
Alderman Myers and Alder Trueblood both strongly advocated for action on the fleet facility, with Trueblood noting he has been on council eight years and the issue has been discussed for six of them with no resolution. A former infrastructure committee member noted the city has already spent $100,000-$200,000 on building plans and that much legwork is done, offering to attend future meetings to provide history on the project. The consensus among speakers was that the fleet facility must be completed this term rather than waiting for a potential county site to open up.

### Economic Development, TIDs/TIFs, and Housing
Alder Neal delivered an extended argument that council members and the public fundamentally misunderstand TIF and TID tools, warning that characterizing developer incentives as 'taxpayer money going to rich people' is inaccurate and puts Wausau at a competitive disadvantage. Alder Kilian countered that high market-rate rents in new apartment buildings are unaffordable for workers earning $20-$22 per hour, and called for more education so residents understand how these projects are financed. Alder Trueblood advocated for housing at all levels including market rate, affordable, single-family, duplexes, and accessory dwelling units, noting Wausau's housing stock is old and land for expansion is limited. Mayor Diny noted that developers build what generates ROI and the city's role is to work with developers, not act as one.

### Youth Retention and Community Vibrancy
Alder Neal argued forcefully that attracting and retaining youth should be a top-line strategic priority, stating 'if we don't have a plan and if we aren't actively doing everything we can to attract and retain our youth here, our schools are emptying out.' He noted Wausau school enrollment has declined from roughly 8,400 to 7,400 students since 2000. Alder Tierney raised the idea of things for teens to do that don't cost money and mentioned reviving a mayor's youth council. Mayor Diny referenced Bentonville, Arkansas as a model community that transformed itself into a destination through trail systems and amenities, reversing talent drain.

### Civic Engagement and Communication
One alder raised the issue of profound civic disengagement, noting that council and committee meetings routinely have empty houses, YouTube analytics on city meetings are very low, and the city lacks a centralized, cohesive communication strategy across departments. Alder Kilian described success with a neighborhood group in her district that now draws a packed room monthly. Multiple council members discussed the need for plain-language videos explaining tax bills and budget, with one alder describing spending hours with city staff to understand the revaluation before being able to explain it to constituents.

### Transit Expansion
Alder Trueblood called Metro Ride the one thing Wausau has that no other community has and said it must be leveraged. Mayor Diny noted new transit director Ron Schunky is taking a new approach to regional expansion—selling transit to neighboring communities based on benefit to them rather than cost—and cited a new Northern Valley route serving 40-plus riders per day as early progress. Alder Luken advocated for expanding public transportation routes and times to serve residents who cannot afford personal vehicles, particularly to connect workers to local and regional employers.

### Overtime and Event Costs
Alder Tierney raised the issue of Wausau taxpayers funding police and ambulance overtime for county-wide events such as the fair, Art in the Park, and the upcoming air show, and suggested Marathon County share those costs. Another alder responded that the overtime issue is more nuanced than it appears, citing meetings held with the mayor, police chief, fire chief, and DPW, and announced an HR committee meeting on July 13th where all department heads will be invited to address overtime on the record. Council members were invited to submit questions in advance.

### PFAS Litigation Update
Alder Tierney raised the lack of public follow-up on the city's PFAS lawsuits, noting residents don't know what settlement money has been received or how it's being used. The city attorney confirmed payments have been received from 3M, Tyco, BASF, and DuPont over a settlement structure that will continue for nine years, and that a special needs claim is still pending for this summer. The attorney stated no settlement money has been allocated to anything yet and committed to preparing a confidential litigation summary update. Council members requested a finance department accounting of how funds are tracked.

### Priority Dot Voting and Motion to Compile Results
Council members placed dot stickers on posted priority items organized under strategic plan headers. Because the exercise produced a complex visual result rather than a clear top three, a motion was made that staff coordinator Michelle compile all the information and send it to the council president by end of week, at which point the council president would determine how to distribute it for formal action. The motion was seconded and passed unanimously with no opposition.

## Public Comment
One member of the public was present but did not offer formal comment. Alder Neal acknowledged the visitor and invited him to contact council members individually. The visitor briefly mentioned interest in greening the city and innovative environmental remediation approaches when acknowledged by council.
