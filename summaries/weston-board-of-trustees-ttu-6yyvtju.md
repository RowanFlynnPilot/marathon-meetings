## Meeting Overview
The Weston Village Board of Trustees held a wide-ranging meeting covering several significant decisions, most notably approving the Mountain Bay Metropolitan Police Department building contract with 8 Pine Inc. for $6.7 million — coming in under a $7 million budget — and receiving a detailed presentation from DC Everest school district leaders on long-range facility planning that could include a $81–94 million referendum in April 2027. The board also discussed SAFER fire staffing, public works challenges including asbestos pipe replacement and utility operator turnover, and various committee reports.

## Key Discussions
### Public Comment
Jim Pinsel addressed the board on three topics: (1) strong opposition to removing legal details from the Finance and HR Committee's monthly review, arguing it enables 'swampy underhanded backroom deals' and citing $10,000 in legal fees he said were used to avoid public records disclosure; (2) support for the police department building but urging that the $362,354.32 in returned EMPD funds be exclusively earmarked to reduce borrowing for the new building; and (3) a call for the board to set 2027 budget goals including fully funding the fire department and minimizing property taxes.

### DC Everest Long-Range Facility Planning Presentation
Superintendent Dr. Casey Nye and Assistant Superintendent Dr. Kelly Strike presented DC Everest's long-range facility plan, which involves decommissioning the 75-year-old junior high, moving 8th graders to the middle school (creating a 6–8 building) and 9th graders to the high school (creating a 9–12 building), with an estimated cost of $81–94 million plus potential additional costs if a new aquatics facility is added. The district is targeting an April 2027 referendum, noting that current enrollment has declined from about 5,900 five years ago to 5,730, and that the 2018 referendum passed with roughly 70.8% approval. Trustees asked about the operational costs of retaining the junior high structure partially, staffing reductions (attrition-based, no planned layoffs), the age of other facilities, and the potential pool project near GTCC. Dr. Nye acknowledged the pool decision is exploratory and that community partnership conversations are ongoing.

### Mountain Bay Metro Police Department Bid Results (Item 54)
Chief Mark Kern presented bid results for the new Mountain Bay Metropolitan Police Department building: the base bid from contractor 8 Pine Inc. came in at $6,390,100, with the total project including fiber optic line, lockers, furniture, and security estimated at $6.7 million — under the $7 million budget. Five bid alternatives were considered; three were eliminated. The approximately 26,000 sq. ft. building includes a 13-squad garage expandable during adverse weather. Trustee Katrina Clark expressed disappointment at the process that led to needing a new building, saying she felt misled when the merger was approved after being told existing facilities were adequate; Chief Kern took personal responsibility, apologizing to the board. Finance administrator Ryan noted an intergovernmental agreement with Rothschild must be finalized in August before the contract with 8 Pine can be signed; 8 Pine indicated it could begin construction by end of September. Weston's share is approximately $4,355,000 (65%), with Rothschild covering 35%. The board voted unanimously 7-0 to approve the contract.

### Committee Reports — Finance and HR
Trustee Barbara Ermeling reported the committee discussed the Cintas agreement and an annual exit interview report showing 7.5% village-wide staff turnover, which she characterized as a strong result for a public entity. The committee also discussed ways elected officials can show appreciation to staff, with follow-up planned for August.

### Department Reports — Public Works
Director Michael reported the Alderson-Gelnik roundabout is progressing with curb and gutter poured and sidewalk work beginning; utility pole removal was delayed until after the World Cup finale due to telecom contract restrictions. A utility operator gave two-week notice — the third consecutive year of turnover in that position around the one-year mark — raising concerns about operational impact and training burden. Discussion also covered the asbestos pipe replacement program (10 miles in the village, with a grant being pursued), street lighting gaps near the river and along Score Avenue to campus, and the long-term future of the bridge over the Oclair River on Ross Avenue. Trustees also discussed the planned Ross Avenue and Camp Phillips roundabout, driven by crash analysis and a traffic study showing the intersection trending toward level-of-service F by 2034.

### Resolution 2026-019: Final Plat of Bull Junior View Subdivision
Trustee Luis Lopes-Serrao moved and Trustee Katrina Clark seconded approval of the final plat for Bull Junior View Subdivision (Project 20250424, Reading Builders, Freeland). The motion passed unanimously with no discussion.

### SAFER Fire Staffing (Item 37)
Village President Maloney and others briefly discussed the SAFER board's approval of adding three staff for 2027, which the board indicated they have comfort funding via TIF district closure revenue without a referendum. Maloney expressed the need for a longer-term staffing plan — potentially adding two more in year two and three more in year three — to be discussed during budget season, noting the public's rejection of the fire fee referendum signaled they do not want another referendum approach.

## Public Comment
One speaker offered public comment: Jim Pinsel, who identified himself and gave his address. He raised three issues: opposition to removing legal details from the Finance and HR Committee's monthly report, urging that $362,354.32 in returned EMPD funds be exclusively earmarked to reduce borrowing for the new police building rather than diverted to other uses, and calling on the board to set specific 2027 budget goals including fully funding the fire department and minimizing property tax increases.
